Reason-Guessr: Bridging the Gap Between Pixel Classification and Geographic Reasoning

1. Home

The Problem

The core challenge is Image-Based Geolocation (IBG): determining the exact geographic coordinates (latitude/longitude) of a location solely from a single image. This problem is popularized by the game GeoGuessr, where players are dropped into a random Google Street View location and must deduce their whereabouts.

Unlike standard object recognition, IBG requires multi-level understanding of visual context:

  • Global Cues: Climate, vegetation (flora), and architectural styles
  • Local Cues: Road markings, license plate colors, utility pole designs, and language on signs
  • Knowledge Retrieval: Associating these cues with a global mental map

The Black-Box Problem

The traditional approach to image-based geolocation is to train a CNN (convolutional neural network) as a large-scale classifier: the world is divided into thousands of geographic grid cells, and the CNN learns to predict which cell an image belongs to. Systems like PlaNet and IM2GPS follow this paradigm and achieve coarse localization, but remain fundamentally black boxes. When the model is wrong, there is no explanation, no reasoning chain, and no way to improve without more labeled data.

Figure 1: The black-box problem: CNN outputs a grid cell prediction with no explanation

  • No explanation for predictions
  • Memorizes textures, not geography
  • Brittle to new regions

  • 99% train / 33% test accuracy gap

Explicit reasoning over visual cues

  • Notice language on street signs
  • Identify architectural style
  • Recognize vegetation patterns
  • Check license plate colors
  • Map cues to world knowledge

Our Solution: Reason-Guessr

We ask: can we teach a model to reason the same way humans do? Rather than adopting a single approach, this project systematically explores multiple methods that each attempt to add more context and interpretability to this black-box problem. Our methodology follows a three-step progression:

  1. Step 1 - Replicate the CNN Baseline: We reproduce the ResNeXt-101 classification pipeline on our self-collected dataset, understand where and why it fails (overfitting, memorization), and establish performance bounds.
  2. Step 2 - Adapt a CLIP-ViT Backbone: We replace the CNN with a CLIP ViT-B/32 encoder pretrained on 400M image-text pairs. CLIP's geographic priors reduce overfitting and improve generalization without requiring more labeled data.
  3. Step 3 - Reasoning-Enhanced CLIP (Reason-Guessr): We add a zero-shot geographic cue extraction layer on top of CLIP, probing for interpretable signals (climate zone, license plate type, vegetation, architecture) and fusing them with visual embeddings for direct coordinate regression.

The result is Reason-Guessr: a system that bridges pixel classification and geographic reasoning, combining the semantic knowledge of vision-language models with structured, human-interpretable cues.

Why This Matters

Image-based geolocation has significant real-world applications:

  • Media Verification: Fact-checking the origin of photos and videos to combat misinformation
  • Disaster Response: Localizing social media images during emergencies to support rescue coordination
  • Cybersecurity & Privacy: Understanding how AI can infer sensitive locations from personal photos
  • Autonomous Systems: Visual localization for drones, autonomous vehicles, and embodied AI agents

Beyond applications, IBG represents a shift from pure pixel classification toward structured visual reasoning. Our approach demonstrates that explicit geographic reasoning (extracting cues like climate zones, license plate colors, and architectural styles) can be integrated into end-to-end prediction pipelines, improving both accuracy and interpretability.

2. Related Work

Early Approaches: Grid Classification

PlaNet [Weyand et al., 2016] was the first to tackle this at scale, dividing Earth into thousands of S2 grid cells and training a CNN to classify images. It achieved coarse localization but struggled with fine-grained precision. IM2GPS [Vo & Hays, 2017] improved with better CNN backbones (ResNet) and larger datasets, but maintained the same black-box classification paradigm.

Vision Transformers

ViT [Dosovitskiy et al., 2021] brought self-attention to computer vision, enabling better global context modeling. When applied to geolocation, ViTs improved accuracy but still functioned as improved classifiers rather than reasoning systems, with no interpretable intermediate outputs.

Vision-Language Models

CLIP [Radford et al., 2021] changed the landscape by learning joint image-text embeddings on 400M image-caption pairs from the web. Because CLIP was trained on natural language descriptions of scenes, it internalized rich geographic priors, recognizing architectural styles, vegetation types, and cultural markers without explicit geographic supervision.

Recent CLIP-Based Work

GeorAIn [Anwar, 2025] showed that a frozen CLIP ViT-B/16 with just a two-layer MLP head achieves 489km mean error and 103km median error across 23 cities, trained on only 113K images, demonstrating that CLIP's pretrained features carry substantial geographic information without massive task-specific datasets.

PIGEON [Haas et al., 2024] uses a coarse-to-fine pipeline with contrastive pretraining on millions of geotagged images, then refines predictions with a retrieval module. It uses a CLIP backbone as its foundation and achieves state-of-the-art results (44.4 km median error globally), but requires massive compute and data, training on 4.7M images across 100 epochs.

The OpenStreetView-5M benchmark [Astruc et al., 2024] confirmed that classification-augmented methods consistently outperform pure coordinate regression, which motivated our hybrid approach combining classification and explicit cue extraction.

Why These Constraints Shaped Our Approach

State-of-the-art methods like PIGEON require 4.7 million geotagged images and substantial multi-GPU compute infrastructure. With a self-collected dataset of 8,000 locations and a Google Colab Pro GPU budget, replicating such pipelines is infeasible. Our constraints shaped our design choices directly:

  • Dataset size (~8,000 locations): Too small for end-to-end fine-tuning of large backbones without severe overfitting. This pushed us toward frozen or partially-frozen CLIP encoders as built-in regularizers.
  • Compute budget (Colab A100): Rules out training from scratch or multi-stage retrieval pipelines. We needed efficient inference, which CLIP's zero-shot cue extraction provides at roughly 22 locations per second.
  • Single-country scope: Croatia provides a geographically coherent benchmark with validated open-source competition code to adapt, rather than building a pipeline from scratch.

Our Contribution

While prior work either (1) treats geolocation as pure classification with no interpretability, or (2) uses VLMs to extract cues but does not integrate them into predictions, we bridge that gap: zero-shot cue extraction fused end-to-end with CLIP visual embeddings for direct coordinate regression, combining the semantic reasoning of VLMs with the prediction accuracy of learned models.

3. Dataset

Google Street View Collection

We built a Croatia-focused dataset using the Google Street View Static API. We sampled 8,000 random coordinates within Croatia's bounding box (latitude 42.4–46.5°N, longitude 13.5–19.4°E), verified Street View coverage via the metadata API, and downloaded four cardinal-direction images (0°, 90°, 180°, 270°) per valid location at 640×640 resolution.

This yielded 32,000 images across 8,000 unique locations, partitioned into 31 geographic classes using Voronoi tessellation at 0.5-degree spacing. The dataset was split 90/5/5 into train (~7,200 locations), validation (~400), and test (~400). After filtering out the 104 UUID folders missing one or more cardinal-direction images, the held-out test set contains 257 locations.

Figure 2: Croatia partitioned into 31 geographic classes using Voronoi tessellation (0.5-degree spacing)
Figure 3: Four cardinal views (0°, 90°, 180°, 270°) from a single Croatia location

Why Four Views?

Human GeoGuessr players don't make judgments from a single fixed viewpoint; they "look around" to gather contextual clues. The four cardinal views replicate this: one direction might show a license plate, another might capture a road sign with text, another might reveal architectural details. Aggregating these views via mean-pooling gives the model a richer spatial understanding of each location.

4. Approach

Our methodology follows a three-phase progression from traditional CNN classification to zero-shot reasoning.

Phase 1: CNN Baseline (ResNeXt-101)

We adapted the ciglenecki/ml-competition-cv-geoguessr pipeline as our baseline. Croatia is partitioned into 31 geographic grid cells at 0.7° spacing. A ResNeXt-101 (32×8d) CNN pretrained on ImageNet classifies street-level images into these cells.

Architecture: Each location provides four cardinal-direction images (N/E/S/W). Each image is passed through the ResNeXt-101 backbone to extract a 2048-d feature vector. The four vectors are concatenated (4 × 2048 = 8192-d), then passed through a fully-connected classifier to predict one of 31 grid classes. The predicted class centroid becomes the final lat/lng estimate.

Training: Cross-entropy loss, Adam optimizer (lr=1e-4), OneCycleLR scheduler, 15 epochs, batch size 16. Backbone frozen for first 3 epochs, then unfrozen for fine-tuning.

Phase 2: CLIP Classification

We replace the ResNeXt backbone with a CLIP ViT-B/32 encoder. Because CLIP was trained on 400M image-text pairs scraped from the internet, it has internalized rich geographic priors, associating visual patterns with semantic concepts like "Mediterranean architecture" or "Alpine vegetation", without any explicit geolocation supervision.

Architecture: Same 4-view setup. Each image is resized to 224x224 and passed through CLIP ViT-B/32's vision encoder, which divides the image into 7x7 = 49 patches and processes them through 12 transformer layers with 768-dimensional embeddings. The pooler_output [CLS] token is a 768-d embedding per image. The four embeddings are concatenated (4 x 768 = 3,072-d), then passed through a linear classification head (trainable from init, ~95K parameters) to predict one of 31 geographic classes.

Training strategy: The backbone is fully frozen for the first 2 epochs (training only the linear head), then the last 4 transformer layers are unfrozen for fine-tuning. This staged approach balances retaining CLIP's pretrained geographic knowledge with task-specific adaptation. CLIP ViT-B/32 has ~87.5M parameters, comparable to ResNeXt-101's 88M, but its geographic pretraining rather than ImageNet pretraining is what drives the accuracy gain. Training ran for 103 epochs on a Colab A100 (fp16 mixed precision) in approximately 2 hours, with the best checkpoint selected by validation haversine distance.

Phase 3: Reason-Guessr (Zero-Shot Cue Fusion)

The full system extracts interpretable geographic cues zero-shot using CLIP's image-text alignment, then fuses them with the visual embedding to regress lat/lng directly, bypassing grid discretization entirely.

Step 1: Zero-Shot Cue Extraction

We define 6 geographic cue categories, each with 3-4 class labels. For each class, 2-4 natural-language prompt templates are authored and averaged (L2-normalized) into a single class embedding:

Cue CategoryClasses
License Platewhite EU, yellow UK, white US
Climate ZoneMediterranean, continental, tropical, arid
Language ScriptLatin, Cyrillic, Arabic, Asian
Road InfrastructureEuropean, North American, developing
Architecture StyleMediterranean, North European, East European, modern
Vegetation Typeconiferous, deciduous, Mediterranean, tropical

For each location, we:

  1. Encode each of the 4 cardinal-direction images through CLIP ViT-B/32 and mean-pool into a single 512-d location embedding
  2. Compute cosine similarity between the location embedding and each class embedding
  3. For each category, take the argmax prediction; raw similarity scores give per-class confidence
  4. One-hot encode the 6 predictions → 22-d sparse vector (3+4+4+3+4+4 = 22 dimensions)

The full pipeline processed all 8,000 Croatia locations (32,000 images) on a Colab T4 GPU at ~22 locations/second, completing in approximately 6 minutes.

Figure 4: Zero-shot cue extraction on coastal Croatia location with confidence scores

Example: For a coastal Dalmatian location, CLIP might extract:

  • Climate: "Coastal Adriatic climate" (confidence: 0.61)
  • Plates: "EU white license plates" (confidence: 0.36)
  • Vegetation: "Mediterranean scrubland" (confidence: 0.49)
  • Architecture: "Mediterranean coastal houses" (confidence: 0.51)
  • Road Infra: "Coastal scenic route" (confidence: 0.28)
  • Language: "Croatian Latin script signs" (confidence: 0.09)

Step 2: Fusion MLP

We concatenate the 22-d cue vector with the 512-d CLIP visual embedding (mean-pooled across 4 views) to form a 534-d feature vector. This is passed through a 2-layer MLP with ReLU activations to regress directly to (lat, lng) coordinates with MSE loss. For MLP training and evaluation the dataset is split 80/20, with 1,600 held-out test locations.

Figure 5: 2-layer fusion MLP architecture

Why this works: The visual embedding captures low-level spatial patterns, while the cue vector provides high-level semantic structure. By fusing both, the MLP learns to map "Mediterranean climate + coastal architecture + EU plates" to specific coordinate ranges, mimicking human reasoning.

5. Challenges

Infrastructure & Training Setup

Hardware: Google Colab with NVIDIA A100 GPU (40GB VRAM). All training runs completed in <6 hours.

Frameworks: PyTorch 2.0, PyTorch Lightning 2.1, TorchVision 0.15, Transformers 4.36 (for CLIP), OpenCV 4.8.

Hyperparameters: Batch size 16, learning rate 1e-4 (frozen backbone) / 1e-5 (fine-tuning), weight decay 1e-3, OneCycleLR → ReduceLROnPlateau scheduler, 15 epochs total (CNN baseline) / 10 epochs (CLIP).

Technical Bugs Fixed

The baseline repository (ciglenecki/ml-competition-cv-geoguessr) was built in 2021 and required extensive debugging to run on modern environments:

1. Broken symlinks: The preprocessing script created symlinks from /content/dataset/train/<uuid>/ to /content/drive/MyDrive/.../images/<uuid>/ instead of copying files. This caused FileNotFoundError mid-epoch when Colab's Drive mount timed out. Fix: Added --copy-images flag to physically copy all files to local disk before training.

2. Incomplete UUIDs: 104 out of 8,000 UUID folders were missing one of the 4 directional images (e.g., had 0.jpg, 90.jpg, 180.jpg but not 270.jpg). These caused crashes during batch loading. Fix: Filtered CSV to exclude incomplete locations before generating train/val/test splits.

3. CSV/split mismatch: The rich static CSV (data_rich_static__spacing_0.5_classes_55.csv) referenced 8,000 UUIDs, but only 3,715 actually existed on disk after preprocessing. Training silently used the intersection, reducing effective dataset size. Fix: Manual CSV regeneration to match actual on-disk UUIDs.

4. Code compatibility bugs:

  • IndentationError in train.py line 203: missing indent after conditional
  • KeyError: 'has_image' in preprocess_csv_create_rich_static.py: DataFrame column did not exist in newer pandas
  • TypeError from OneCycleLR(verbose=True): parameter deprecated in PyTorch 2.0
  • DeprecationWarning for pretrained=True, migrated to weights=ResNeXt101_32X8D_Weights.DEFAULT

5. Colab-specific constraints: num_workers=0 required to prevent DataLoader multiprocessing deadlocks on Colab's sandboxed environment. Dataset workflow: zip on Google Drive → copy single .zip to /content/ → extract locally to leverage SSD speed (10x faster than Drive symlinks).

6. PyTorch 2.6 weights_only=True default: PyTorch 2.6 changed torch.load to use weights_only=True by default. Checkpoints saved by the repository contain sklearn MinMaxScaler and numpy array objects not in PyTorch's safe globals list. This broke both training resumption and test evaluation. Fix: monkey-patch torch.load at the top of train.py before any Lightning code runs, setting weights_only=False for trusted internal checkpoints. A named function (not a lambda) is required to prevent infinite recursion on Colab cell re-runs.

7. Empty validation dataloader with drop_last=True: The repository sets drop_last=True by default. With small validation subsets and certain batch sizes, the val dataloader produced zero batches, causing ReduceLROnPlateau to crash because the monitored val/loss_epoch metric never logged. Fix: override drop_last=False specifically in the val and test dataloaders.

8. Incorrect CLIP embedding dimension: Initial implementation assumed CLIP ViT-B/32's pooler_output was 512-dimensional (matching CLIP's projected embedding after the projection head). The raw CLIPVisionModel pooler_output is 768-dimensional. This caused a shape mismatch (16x3072 vs 2048x27) that only surfaced at the ModelSummary step. Fix: corrected CLIP_VARIANTS to use 768 for ViT-B/32; rebuilt the fc layer explicitly after super().__init__().

9. Colab session persistence: Google Colab resets /content/ on disconnect, losing the cloned repository and all installed packages. We implemented a startup.sh script stored on Google Drive that reinstalls packages and re-clones the repository from GitHub. CLIP weights are cached to Drive via HF_HOME environment variable so the 600MB download is not repeated each session.

Discussion

Why CNNs Overfit

The ResNeXt-101 baseline achieved 99.4% train accuracy but only 33.2% test accuracy, a 66-point overfitting gap. With 88M parameters and only ~7,200 training examples across 31 classes (roughly 160 examples per class), the model does not learn geographic features; it memorizes training images. ImageNet pretraining provides no geographic prior, so all geolocation signal must be learned from scratch from the training set alone.

Evidence: when we inspected misclassified test images, the CNN often predicted the training location most visually similar to the test image, rather than the geographically correct grid cell. It learned "this house texture belongs to cell 12" instead of "cell 12 covers the Istrian peninsula."

CLIP ViT-B/32 reduces the train/test gap to 25.6pp (63.1% vs 37.5%), less than half the baseline's gap. Its geographic pretraining on 400M image-text pairs means geographic signal is already encoded in the features; the linear head only needs to map that signal to the 31 Croatian classes. CLIP also improves test accuracy by 4.3 percentage points (+1 in 23 correctly-classified test images) and reduces test loss by 21.8% (2.062 vs 2.638). Lower test loss means CLIP's probability distributions are better calibrated: when wrong, it assigns more probability mass to geographically adjacent classes rather than arbitrary distant ones.

The Haversine Metric Paradox

The scaled haversine numbers in the table above appear to favor ResNeXt-101 (0.01305 vs 0.01494 for CLIP). This result must be interpreted with caution. These values are in normalized coordinate units after MinMaxScaler transformation, not kilometers. A difference of 0.00189 in normalized units corresponds to roughly 0.9-1.1 km in real distance. More critically, the baseline's low haversine score was achieved while severely overfit: its 66-point train/test accuracy gap indicates it partially memorized training locations, and test examples that overlap geographically with memorized training images will appear close in haversine distance through memorization rather than understanding. CLIP's smaller overfitting gap reflects genuine generalization.

Why Language Cue Failed

CLIP achieved only 2.3% accuracy on language/OCR cues, the worst by far. 96% of locations were predicted as "Cyrillic script" when they actually contained Croatian Latin script. Two causes:

  1. CLIP is weak at OCR. It was trained on image-caption pairs, not text recognition. Text in street signs is small, rotated, partially occluded, conditions where CLIP's patch-based ViT struggles.
  2. Mean-pooling destroys text. Averaging four distinct images blurs fine-grained details. One view might have a clear street sign, but averaging it with three blank walls makes the text unreadable.

Future fix: Replace mean-pooling with cross-attention, allowing the model to focus on the single view containing readable text. Or integrate a dedicated OCR model (Tesseract, PaddleOCR) as a separate module.

Key Finding: Symmetric Prompt Collapse

While debugging the two failed cues (road infrastructure and language script), we discovered a systematic failure in CLIP's text encoder. When we measured cosine similarity between opposing class embeddings, the results were alarming:

Prompt PairCosine Similarity
"driving on the left" vs "driving on the right"0.99
"latin script" vs "cyrillic script"0.94
"european road" vs "north-american road"0.91

Symmetric templates (e.g., "...on the left" vs "...on the right") share 90%+ of their tokens. The distinguishing token ("left" vs "right") contributes only a small fraction of the final sentence embedding. CLIP's text encoder collapses opposing classes into nearly-identical vectors, making classification a coin flip biased by embedding geometry rather than visual content.

Fix: Differential Prompts. Replacing symmetric templates with prompts that describe visually distinct features reduces the collapse. For example:

  • Before: "a photo of cars driving on the LEFT side of the road"
  • After: "a car with the steering wheel on the RIGHT, driving in the left lane"

The differential prompt encodes a visually distinguishable feature (steering wheel position) rather than a spatial label, reducing pairwise text embedding similarity from 0.99 to 0.75. This finding generalizes beyond geolocation to any CLIP-based categorical classifier where class labels are spatially or directionally symmetric.

Data Size as Primary Bottleneck

Our clearest result: more data > better hyperparameters. Doubling the dataset from ~2,500 to ~5,000 locations nearly doubled test accuracy (10.9% to 21.4%). The full 8,000-location dataset pushed it to 33.2%.

The original ciglenecki competition used 16,000+ locations. PIGEON trained on 4.7M images. Our 8,000 is still data-starved (~160 examples per class), which explains why CLIP's geographic features cannot yet be fully exploited. Scaling to OpenStreetView-5M (4.7M global images) would likely push CLIP classification accuracy well above 50%.

Future Work

  • Scale the dataset: Download 20,000+ Croatia locations via Google Street View API. Extend to global coverage with OpenStreetView-5M or MSLS datasets.
  • Replace mean-pooling with attention: Use a Transformer encoder to aggregate the 4 views, allowing the model to attend to the most informative view (e.g., the one with a readable street sign) rather than averaging blindly.
  • Integrate dedicated OCR: Use Tesseract or EasyOCR to extract text from signs, then verify semantic meaning with CLIP ("Is this Croatian Latin or Cyrillic?").
  • Fine-tune CLIP selectively: Unfreeze only the last 4 transformer layers instead of the entire backbone. This balances semantic knowledge retention with task-specific adaptation.
  • Multi-country evaluation: Within Croatia, climate and plate colors are near-constants and cannot discriminate between locations. Testing on a multi-country dataset (e.g., Croatia vs Italy vs Slovenia) would better demonstrate the value of cue-based reasoning, where cues like license plate color and language script vary meaningfully across borders.
  • Multimodal LLM integration: Use GPT-4V or LLaVA to generate free-form chain-of-thought reasoning ("I see Austro-Hungarian architecture, yellow license plates, and Mediterranean vegetation; this is likely coastal Croatia near Rijeka") before coordinate prediction.
